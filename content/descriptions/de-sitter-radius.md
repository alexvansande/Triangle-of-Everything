---
title: De Sitter Radius
---

The de Sitter radius marks the ultimate size limit of the observable universe. In a cosmos dominated by dark energy, the expansion rate approaches a constant value (the Hubble parameter H∞), and the Hubble radius converges to c/H∞ — approximately 1.6 × 10²⁸ cm.

This is barely larger than the current Hubble radius (1.37 × 10²⁸ cm), meaning the triangle of possible objects is already nearly at its final size. As the universe ages, no new objects will become visible. Instead, distant galaxies will gradually recede beyond this horizon and fade from view.

Anything beyond the de Sitter radius recedes from us faster than the speed of light. Not because objects move through space faster than light, but because space itself expands. Light emitted from beyond this boundary will never reach us — those regions are permanently inaccessible.

In the far future, the observable universe will appear increasingly empty. Only gravitationally bound structures — like our Local Group of galaxies — will remain visible. Everything else will have slipped beyond the horizon forever.
