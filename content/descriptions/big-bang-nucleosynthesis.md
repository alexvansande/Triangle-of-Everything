The epoch from $\sim 10^{-6}$ s to $\sim 3$ minutes after the [[Big Bang]], during which the lightest atomic nuclei were forged. As the universe cools below $\sim 10^9$ K, [[Proton & Neutron|protons and neutrons]] begin fusing into light elements: primarily hydrogen ($^1$H), helium-4 ($^4$He, about 25% by mass), plus trace amounts of deuterium ($^2$H), helium-3, and lithium-7. The predicted abundances from BBN match observations with remarkable precision, providing one of the strongest confirmations of the hot Big Bang model. The process is self-limiting: by the time heavier nuclei could form, the universe has expanded and cooled too much for further fusion. The primordial composition — roughly 75% hydrogen and 25% helium — persists until the first stars ignite hundreds of millions of years later, forging heavier elements through stellar nucleosynthesis.
