The energy scale of $\sim 10^{-3}$ eV (millielectronvolts) characterizing the binding energy of Cooper pairs — correlated electron pairs that carry current with zero electrical resistance. In conventional (BCS) superconductors, phonon-mediated attraction between electrons opens a gap $\Delta \sim 1$–$10$ meV in the energy spectrum, with the critical temperature given by $k_B T_c \sim \Delta$. Below $T_c$, the material enters a macroscopic quantum state that expels magnetic fields (the Meissner effect) and permits persistent currents. High-temperature cuprate superconductors operate at somewhat higher energies ($\sim 10^{-2}$ eV), with pairing mechanisms that remain one of the outstanding puzzles in condensed matter physics.
