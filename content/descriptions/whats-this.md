This is a graph plotting all objects by mass and radius. It's a log scale, meaning every square is a 10× increase in either mass (vertical axis) or width (horizontal axis).

A cube of water 10 cm wide is exactly 1 liter, which is 1 kilogram – this is not a coincidence but how the metric system was defined in the early days. A sphere of water of 1 liter would be a little bit wider, but as you can see, in a log scale these differences are imperceptible, so we can use width and diameter interchangeably for the most part.

If we increase the width 10×, we get a cube of 1 meter on each side, which increases the volume 1000×. Because water density is exactly 1 g/cm³, it also means increasing the weight 1000×. The blue diagonal therefore is the density of water, and it shouldn't be surprising that most things we deal with in our universe are around that density. Anything to the right of the line floats on water; anything to the left will sink.

[[Hippopotamus|Hippos]], surprisingly, are denser than water and can't swim – they walk underwater. [[Jupiter]], on the other hand, is actually as dense as a pool noodle and would float in water — if you had a big enough pool!
