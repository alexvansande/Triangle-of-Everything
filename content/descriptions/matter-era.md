The epoch following [[Cosmic Microwave Background|recombination]], spanning from $\sim 380{,}000$ years to the present day ($13.8$ billion years). After photons decouple from matter and the universe becomes transparent, gravity takes center stage: small density fluctuations — visible as tiny temperature variations in the CMB — grow into the cosmic web of filaments, clusters, and voids.

The first few hundred million years are the cosmic Dark Ages, with no stars or luminous sources. Around $100$–$200$ million years, the first stars and galaxies ignite, flooding the universe with ultraviolet light that reionizes the intergalactic medium. Over billions of years, galaxies merge and evolve, heavy elements are forged in stellar cores and supernovae, and planetary systems — including our own — form from enriched gas and dust.

For most of this era, matter ($\Omega_m$) dominates the energy budget and expansion decelerates. But around $9.8$ billion years after the [[Big Bang]] ($\sim 4$ billion years ago), dark energy ($\Omega_\Lambda$) overtakes matter, and the expansion of the universe begins to accelerate — the transition into the current [[Dark Energy Era|dark energy]] dominated phase.
