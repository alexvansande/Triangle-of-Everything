The current and final known epoch of cosmic evolution, beginning roughly 9.8 billion years after the [[Big Bang]] (about 4 billion years ago). As the universe expanded and matter diluted, the cosmological constant — a form of energy intrinsic to empty space — came to dominate the total energy budget. Unlike matter or radiation, dark energy density remains approximately constant as space expands, driving an accelerating expansion discovered in 1998 through observations of distant Type Ia supernovae by the Supernova Cosmology Project and the High-z Supernova Search Team. Today dark energy accounts for roughly 68% of the universe's total energy content, with dark matter at ~27% and ordinary matter at ~5%. If dark energy remains constant, the universe will continue expanding at an ever-increasing rate, with distant galaxies eventually receding faster than light and passing beyond the [[Observable Universe|observable horizon]] — leading, over immense timescales, toward a cold, dilute heat death.
